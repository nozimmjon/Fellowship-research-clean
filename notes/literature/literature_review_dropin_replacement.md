### Concepts and Measurement
Intergenerational educational mobility links family background to equality of opportunity and to the allocation of skills. When parental schooling strongly predicts children's attainment, educational outcomes reflect inherited circumstances as well as effort and talent. For that reason, mobility is not only a fairness concern; it is also a compact way to ask whether schooling systems convert demographic growth and human potential into broadly shared opportunity or instead reproduce educational advantage across generations [@torche_2015; @causa_johansson_2010; @neidhoefer_etal_2024].

The literature also cautions against treating any single statistic as a complete description of opportunity. Mobility measures summarize intergenerational association rather than recovering a full structural account of equality of opportunity, and recent evidence shows that cross-country rankings can move materially with coding choices, estimator selection, and sample definitions [@torche_2015; @stromberg_engzell_2025]. For a study built on repeated cross-sections with modest questionnaire differences across waves, that is a direct argument against relying on one headline number.

This paper therefore uses a locked bundle of category-based and rank-based measures. Category transitions retain an intuitive interpretation by asking whether respondents surpass, match, or fall below parental schooling categories. Rank-based measures are better suited to cross-wave comparison because they locate respondents within each wave's educational distribution and are less sensitive to aggregate educational expansion and minor coding differences. The two families answer related but not identical questions: category rates preserve substantive interpretability, whereas rank-based persistence provides the preferred benchmark for comparing waves [@torche_2015; @weide_etal_2024; @stromberg_engzell_2025].

### Global and Developing-Country Evidence
Comparative evidence establishes three regularities. First, educational mobility varies widely across countries and cohorts rather than converging mechanically with development. Second, many developing settings still display lower average mobility than richer economies even after substantial educational expansion. Third, rising schooling access does not automatically weaken intergenerational persistence; more education can coexist with durable family-background gradients in who reaches upper-secondary and tertiary levels [@hertz_2008; @weide_etal_2024; @duong_2024].

This point matters for interpretation. Expansion in average schooling is not itself evidence that opportunity has become less dependent on parental background. Earlier comparative work and more recent evidence from developing regions both show that cohort progress and persistent gradients can coexist for long periods [@chevalier_denny_mcmahon_2009; @razzu_wambile_2022]. For the present paper, the lesson is that Uzbekistan should not be read through a simple modernization narrative in which broader access mechanically implies weaker educational inheritance.

### Transition Economies and Closest Comparators
Transition settings are especially relevant because market liberalization, institutional restructuring, and uneven regional adjustment can alter both schooling opportunity and the household strategies through which education is reproduced. The post-socialist literature does not point to a single mobility path. Comparative evidence shows substantial heterogeneity across former socialist settings, and country studies suggest that common Soviet legacies do not determine later mobility outcomes on their own [@verashchagina_2012; @bruck_esenaliev_2018].

Kyrgyzstan is the closest Central Asian comparator in the published literature cited here, while Turkey offers a second disciplined comparison because it combines middle-income status with strong spatial disparities in school availability, home inputs, and educational inequality. Evidence from Turkey shows that mobility is higher in regions with better schooling opportunity and stronger household resources, which helps motivate the paper's emphasis on region, cohort, and place-based heterogeneity [@aydemir_yazici_2019]. Some comparator studies go further by exploiting exogenous provincial or reform-driven variation in schooling opportunity [@akarcay_polat_2017; @urbina_2018]. Those papers are useful here not because the present study adopts their design, but because they clarify what a genuinely causal architecture would require and why the claims in this paper remain more limited. Broader evidence linking mobility to local development conditions likewise supports the inclusion of region and cohort effects while reinforcing an associational interpretation of the pooled coefficients [@guell_etal_2018; @neidhoefer_etal_2024].

### Uzbekistan's Education Opportunity Structure
Country diagnostics explain why Uzbekistan deserves separate attention rather than inference from neighboring settings or richer-country evidence alone. World Bank and UNICEF assessments point to persistent disparities across region, urban and rural location, gender, tertiary pathways, and the quality of household learning environments [@wb_uzb_2018; @unicef_sitan_2020; @unicef_sitan_2024; @wb_gender_2024]. These sources are not mobility designs, but they establish why parental education, place-based conditions, and household support capacity are plausible correlates of educational mobility in Uzbekistan and why a descriptive benchmark is policy-relevant even without a causal design.

The tertiary margin is especially important for interpretation. World Bank analysis of Uzbekistan's higher-education system highlights long-standing constraints in access, quality, and alignment with labor-market needs, while more recent diagnostics continue to emphasize unequal entry pathways and the strategic importance of tertiary expansion [@wb_modernizing_tertiary_2017; @wb_uzb_2018; @unicef_sitan_2024]. In a setting where bottlenecks remain strongest at advanced levels of schooling, intergenerational persistence may survive even when basic and secondary participation improves. The Uzbek case therefore matters not as anecdotal context, but as a setting in which demographic pressure, regional inequality, and tertiary constraints can keep family background highly salient for educational trajectories.

### Post-2017 Reform Context
Recent mobility patterns in Uzbekistan also need to be interpreted against a changed policy backdrop. Since 2017, the education system has been shaped by a broad reform cycle spanning preschool expansion, sector restructuring, renewed emphasis on quality and management, and major efforts to widen access to tertiary education. The World Bank's education sector analysis described the system as being in flux across subsectors, while more recent UNICEF and partnership documents frame education reform as a continuing national priority [@wb_uzb_2018; @unicef_compact_2023; @unicef_sitan_2024]. These reforms matter for this paper less as a source of clean treatment variation than as a change in the opportunity structure facing cohorts and households. They provide background for reading the most recent wave as emerging from an environment of expanded access and institutional reorganization rather than from a static schooling system. At the same time, the reform literature and official diagnostics continue to distinguish gains in access from the harder problems of quality, equity, and labor-market relevance [@wb_modernizing_tertiary_2017; @wb_uzb_2018]. The descriptive design used here therefore does not attribute observed mobility patterns to the reforms themselves; it treats reform as context that sharpens interpretation of cohort, place, and household differences.

### Identification Limits and Contribution
The literature relevant to this project falls into three broad groups. One documents intergenerational mobility descriptively, using transition matrices, rank correlations, or related persistence measures to summarize how strongly children's schooling tracks parental background. A second identifies causal effects of schooling access or reform by exploiting exogenous variation across place, cohort, or policy exposure. A third, especially after 2020, studies pandemic-era learning disruption and the unequal burden that school closures shifted back onto households [@urbina_2018; @alam_tiwari_2020; @munoz_najar_etal_2021]. These literatures are complementary, but they support different kinds of claims.

This paper belongs mainly in the first category. Its central contribution is descriptive and associational: it estimates levels of educational persistence across waves, then examines how those patterns covary with household and regional characteristics in pooled models. The pandemic literature serves a narrower purpose. Global evidence shows that remote schooling increased the importance of connectivity, devices, caregiver time, and household supervision, while Uzbekistan-specific evidence indicates that learning disruption and recovery were unevenly distributed across students and family circumstances [@alam_tiwari_2020; @munoz_najar_etal_2021; @iqbal_patrinos_2025]. These findings justify a bounded child-module extension asking whether lower-parent-education households report greater disruption, but they do not convert the exercise into a causal study of household mechanisms.

Taken together, the literature leaves a clear empirical gap. There is now a substantial comparative literature on intergenerational educational mobility, a growing body of evidence for developing countries, and a smaller but useful literature on post-socialist and nearby comparator settings. For Uzbekistan, however, the available evidence remains weighted toward sector diagnostics and policy discussion rather than a reproducible multi-wave profile of how educational attainment is transmitted across generations. The contribution of this paper is therefore narrow by design but substantively meaningful: it provides a transparent benchmark for educational mobility in Uzbekistan using a locked bundle of persistence measures, a pooled correlates framework, and a short descriptive child-module extension. Its aim is not to estimate the effects of reform or pandemic exposure, but to place Uzbekistan into the comparative mobility conversation with an empirical profile that is explicit about measurement choices, identification limits, and policy-relevant heterogeneity [@weide_etal_2024; @bruck_esenaliev_2018; @wb_uzb_2018].
