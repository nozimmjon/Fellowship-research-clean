### Literature Review

#### Concepts and measurement
Intergenerational educational mobility matters because it links family background to both equality of opportunity and the allocation of skills. When the association between parents’ and children’s schooling is strong, completed education reflects inherited circumstance as well as individual effort and talent. For that reason, educational mobility is not simply a distributional side issue. It is a compact way to study whether schooling systems convert population growth and human potential into broadly shared opportunity, or instead reproduce educational advantage across generations. Comparative work in economics and sociology treats these links as consequential for fairness, productivity, and long-run development rather than as a narrow stratification outcome alone. citeturn739921view0turn633196search2turn633196search3

That literature also cautions against treating any single mobility statistic as a complete description of opportunity. Torche argues that mobility measures summarize the intergenerational association in schooling but do not themselves recover a full structural account of equality of opportunity, while recent robustness work shows that country rankings can move materially with coding choices, estimators, and sample definitions. Cross-country evidence based on harmonized data likewise underscores that measured mobility depends on whether researchers emphasize educational categories, years of schooling, or relative position in the educational distribution. For a paper based on repeated cross-sections with modest cross-wave questionnaire differences, that is a direct argument for measurement pluralism rather than a single headline number. citeturn739921view0turn633196search2turn633196search10

The empirical strategy in this study therefore uses a locked bundle of category-based and rank-based measures. Category transitions retain an intuitive interpretation by asking whether respondents surpass, match, or fall below parental schooling categories. Rank-based measures are better suited to cross-wave comparison because they locate respondents within each wave’s educational distribution and are less sensitive to shifts in educational expansion. The point is not that one family of measures is superior in the abstract, but that they answer related rather than identical questions. The literature on measurement robustness suggests that this combination is especially appropriate when the goal is to produce a transparent descriptive benchmark rather than a single definitive estimate of mobility. citeturn739921view0turn633196search2turn633196search10

#### Global and developing-country evidence
The broader comparative literature establishes three regularities that motivate the present study. First, educational mobility varies widely across countries and cohorts rather than converging mechanically with economic development. Second, many developing settings continue to display lower average mobility than richer economies, even after substantial expansion in schooling access. Third, educational expansion does not automatically weaken intergenerational persistence; more schooling can coexist with durable family-background gradients in who reaches upper secondary or tertiary levels. These patterns are visible in the earlier comparative literature and are reinforced by the newer global mobility database covering a much wider range of countries. citeturn739921view0turn633196search2turn633196search5

This matters for interpretation. A rise in average schooling or an expansion in formal access does not by itself show that educational opportunity has become less dependent on parental background. Work on Africa and other developing contexts reaches a similar conclusion: cohort progress and persistent intergenerational gradients can coexist for long periods. Recent cross-country research also emphasizes that mobility outcomes are shaped by broader structural conditions, including inequality, education systems, and the interaction between household resources and local opportunity. For the present paper, the lesson is that Uzbekistan should not be evaluated through a simple modernization narrative in which more schooling automatically implies weaker educational inheritance. citeturn739921view0turn633196search3turn633196search6

#### Transition economies and closest comparators
Transition economies are especially relevant because market liberalization, institutional restructuring, and uneven regional adjustment can alter both schooling opportunity and the household strategies through which education is reproduced. The post-socialist literature does not suggest a single mobility path. Comparative evidence points to meaningful heterogeneity across former socialist settings, while country studies show that the interaction between public provision, economic disruption, and regional inequality can move educational persistence in different directions. Kyrgyzstan is the closest published Central Asian comparator in the literature currently assembled for this project, and its experience is useful precisely because it shows that common Soviet legacies do not determine later mobility outcomes on their own. citeturn739921view0turn558044search1turn558044search13

Turkey provides a second disciplined comparator because it combines middle-income status with strong spatial disparities in schooling opportunity and household resources. Evidence from Turkey shows that intergenerational educational mobility is higher in regions with better school availability, stronger home inputs, and lower educational inequality. That is substantively relevant here because it helps motivate the paper’s emphasis on region, cohort, and place-based heterogeneity. At the same time, some comparator studies go beyond descriptive association by exploiting exogenous provincial or reform-driven variation in schooling opportunity. Those papers are useful in this review not because the present study adopts their design, but because they clarify what a genuinely causal architecture would require and why the claims in this paper remain more limited. citeturn739921view0turn558044search2turn558044search8

#### Uzbekistan’s education opportunity structure
The Uzbek case deserves separate attention because institutional diagnostics point to persistent differences in access, progression, and household support capacity that are likely to shape educational mobility. Recent World Bank and UNICEF work documents continuing disparities across region, urban and rural location, gender, and educational stage, with particular concern around transitions into higher education, differences in resource quality, and uneven access to supportive learning environments. These sources are not themselves mobility studies, but they establish why parental education, place-based conditions, and household capacity are plausible correlates of mobility in Uzbekistan. They also make clear why a descriptive mobility profile would be policy-relevant even in the absence of a causal identification strategy. citeturn739921view0turn558044search3turn558044search9

The tertiary margin is especially important for interpretation. World Bank analysis of Uzbekistan’s higher education system highlights long-standing constraints in access, quality, and alignment, while more recent diagnostics continue to emphasize the strategic importance of tertiary expansion and unequal entry pathways. In a context where bottlenecks remain strongest at advanced levels of schooling, intergenerational persistence may survive even when basic and secondary access improve. That is one reason the paper treats educational mobility as a layered outcome shaped not only by school participation in childhood, but also by the ability to convert earlier attainment into later progression. For this study, Uzbekistan matters not as an anecdotal case, but as a setting in which demographic pressure, regional inequality, and tertiary constraints make family background especially salient for educational trajectories. citeturn558044search3turn558044search9turn739921view0

#### Identification limits and the role of pandemic evidence
The literature relevant to this project falls into three broad groups. One group documents intergenerational mobility descriptively, using transition matrices, rank correlations, or related persistence measures to summarize how strongly children’s schooling tracks parental background. A second group identifies causal effects of schooling access or reform by exploiting exogenous variation across place, cohort, or policy exposure. A third group, especially after 2020, studies pandemic-era learning disruption and the unequal burden that school closures shifted back onto households. These literatures are complementary, but they answer different questions and support different kinds of claims. citeturn739921view0turn558044search2turn558044search8

This paper belongs mainly in the first category. Its central contribution is descriptive and associational: it estimates levels of educational persistence across waves, then examines how those patterns covary with household and regional characteristics in pooled models. The comparator reform papers help discipline interpretation by showing what stronger causal designs look like. The pandemic literature serves an even narrower purpose. Global evidence shows that remote schooling increased the importance of connectivity, devices, caregiver time, and household supervision, and Uzbekistan-specific evidence indicates that learning losses and recovery were unevenly distributed across students and family circumstances. Those findings justify a bounded child-module extension asking whether lower-parent-education households report greater disruption, but they do not convert the exercise into a causal study of household mechanisms. citeturn739921view0turn558044search8turn558044search9

#### Gap and contribution
Taken together, the literature leaves a clear empirical gap. There is now a substantial comparative literature on intergenerational educational mobility and a growing body of evidence for developing countries. There is also a smaller but useful literature on post-socialist and nearby comparator settings. For Uzbekistan, however, the available evidence is still weighted toward institutional diagnostics, sector analysis, and policy discussion rather than a reproducible multi-wave profile of how educational attainment is transmitted across generations. That gap matters because country context alone cannot tell us whether educational persistence is weak or strong, whether it is changing across survey waves, or how far observed patterns covary with household and regional conditions. citeturn739921view0turn633196search2turn558044search1turn558044search9

The contribution of this paper is therefore narrow by design but substantively meaningful. It provides a transparent multi-wave benchmark for intergenerational educational mobility in Uzbekistan using a locked bundle of persistence measures, a pooled correlates framework, and a short descriptive child-module extension. Its aim is not to offer a sweeping theory of family change or a broad causal estimate of education reform. Rather, it places Uzbekistan into the comparative mobility conversation with a disciplined empirical profile that is explicit about measurement choices, identification limits, and the kinds of policy-relevant heterogeneity that the data can credibly illuminate. citeturn739921view0turn633196search10turn558044search3
