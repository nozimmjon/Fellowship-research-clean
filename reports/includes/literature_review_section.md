### Concepts and Measurement

Intergenerational educational mobility connects family background to both equality of opportunity and the allocation of skills. When parental schooling strongly predicts children's attainment, educational outcomes reflect inherited circumstances rather than effort or talent alone. Mobility therefore serves as a compact indicator of whether schooling systems convert demographic growth into broadly shared opportunity or reproduce educational advantage across generations [@torche_2015; @causa_johansson_2010; @neidhoefer_etal_2024].

No single statistic fully describes opportunity. Mobility measures summarize intergenerational association; they do not recover a structural account of equality of opportunity. Recent evidence shows that cross-country rankings shift with coding choices, estimator selection, and sample definitions [@torche_2015; @stromberg_engzell_2025]. For a study built on repeated cross-sections with modest questionnaire differences across waves, this is a direct argument against relying on one headline number.

This paper therefore uses a locked bundle of category-based and rank-based measures. Category transitions retain an intuitive interpretation by asking whether respondents surpass, match, or fall below parental schooling categories. Rank-based measures are better suited to cross-wave comparison because they locate respondents within each wave's educational distribution and are less sensitive to aggregate educational expansion and minor coding differences. The two families answer related but not identical questions: category rates preserve substantive interpretability, whereas rank-based persistence provides the preferred benchmark for comparing waves [@torche_2015; @weide_etal_2024; @stromberg_engzell_2025].

### Global Evidence and Developing-Country Patterns

Comparative evidence points to three regularities. First, educational mobility varies widely across countries and cohorts; it does not converge mechanically with development. Second, many developing settings display lower average mobility than richer economies even after substantial educational expansion. Third, rising schooling access does not automatically weaken intergenerational persistence. Broader participation can coexist with durable family-background gradients in who reaches upper-secondary and tertiary levels [@hertz_2008; @narayan_etal_2018; @weide_etal_2024; @duong_2024].

This distinction matters for interpretation. Expansion in average schooling is not evidence that opportunity has become less dependent on parental background. Both earlier comparative work and more recent evidence from developing regions show that cohort progress and persistent gradients can coexist for long periods [@chevalier_denny_mcmahon_2009; @razzu_wambile_2022]. Uzbekistan should not be read through a simple modernization narrative in which broader access mechanically implies weaker educational inheritance.

### Transition Economies, Comparator Settings, and the Uzbek Case

Transition settings are relevant because market liberalization, institutional restructuring, and uneven regional adjustment can alter both schooling opportunity and the household strategies through which education is reproduced. The post-socialist literature does not point to a single mobility path. Comparative evidence shows substantial heterogeneity across former socialist countries, and country studies suggest that common Soviet legacies do not determine later mobility outcomes on their own [@verashchagina_2012; @bruck_esenaliev_2018].

Kyrgyzstan is the closest Central Asian comparator in the published literature, while Turkey provides a second useful comparison because it combines middle-income status with strong spatial disparities in school availability and educational inequality. Evidence from Turkey shows that mobility is higher in places with better schooling opportunity and stronger home resources, which motivates this paper's attention to region, cohort, and place-based heterogeneity [@aydemir_yazici_2019]. Some comparator studies go further and exploit reform-driven or provincial variation in schooling opportunity [@akarcay_polat_2017; @urbina_2018]. Those papers clarify what a genuinely causal architecture would require and why the claims in this manuscript are more limited. Broader evidence linking mobility to local development conditions supports the inclusion of region and cohort effects while reinforcing an associational reading of the pooled coefficients [@guell_etal_2018; @neidhoefer_etal_2024].

Uzbekistan deserves separate attention. World Bank and UNICEF diagnostics point to persistent disparities across region, urban and rural location, gender, tertiary pathways, and the quality of household learning environments [@wb_uzb_2018; @unicef_sitan_2020; @unicef_sitan_2024; @wb_gender_2024]. These sources are not mobility designs, but they establish why parental education, place-based conditions, and household support capacity are plausible correlates of educational mobility in Uzbekistan and why a descriptive benchmark is policy-relevant even without a causal design. The tertiary margin deserves particular attention. World Bank analysis of the higher-education system identifies long-standing constraints in access, quality, governance, and labor-market relevance [@wb_modernizing_tertiary_2017]. Where bottlenecks remain strongest at advanced levels of schooling, intergenerational persistence can survive even as basic and secondary participation improve.

Recent mobility patterns must also be read against a changed policy backdrop. Since 2017, Uzbekistan's education system has moved through a broad reform cycle: preschool expansion, sector restructuring, renewed attention to quality and management, and major efforts to widen tertiary access. Official diagnostics frame this period as one of institutional reorganization and sustained reform priority [@wb_uzb_2018; @unicef_compact_2024; @unicef_sitan_2024]. For this paper, the reform context matters not as a source of clean treatment variation, but because the 2022-23 wave reflects an environment of expanded access and system change. The reform literature continues to distinguish gains in access from the harder problems of quality, equity, and labor-market alignment [@wb_modernizing_tertiary_2017; @wb_uzb_2018]. The manuscript does not attribute observed mobility patterns to reforms; it treats reform as context that sharpens interpretation of cohort, place, and household differences.

### Identification Limits and Contribution

The literature relevant to this project falls into three groups. The first documents intergenerational mobility descriptively, using transition matrices, rank correlations, or related persistence measures. The second identifies causal effects of schooling access or reform by exploiting exogenous variation across place, cohort, or policy exposure. The third, growing rapidly after 2020, studies pandemic-era learning disruption and the unequal burden that school closures shifted back onto households [@urbina_2018; @alam_tiwari_2020; @munoz_najar_etal_2021]. These literatures are complementary but support different kinds of claims.

This paper belongs in the first category. Its core evidence comes from repeated LiTS cross-sections rather than a panel following fixed families over time. It can describe population-level persistence and estimate pooled correlates, but it cannot isolate within-family causal change or identify effects of reforms. The same discipline applies to Module C. Global and Uzbekistan-specific pandemic evidence justifies asking whether lower-parent-education households report greater disruption, but the LiTS IV child module is a small cross-sectional subsample and does not support a causal account of household mechanisms [@alam_tiwari_2020; @munoz_najar_etal_2021; @iqbal_patrinos_2025].

The literature leaves a clear but modest empirical gap. Comparative work on intergenerational educational mobility is extensive, developing-country evidence is growing, and post-socialist comparators are increasingly informative. For Uzbekistan, however, the evidence base remains weighted toward sector diagnostics and policy discussion rather than a reproducible multi-wave profile of how educational attainment is transmitted across generations. The contribution of this paper is narrow by design but substantively useful: it provides a transparent benchmark using a locked bundle of persistence measures, a pooled associational framework, and a short descriptive child-module extension. The aim is not to evaluate reforms or pandemic shocks, but to place Uzbekistan into the comparative mobility conversation with an empirical profile that is explicit about measurement choices, identification limits, and policy-relevant heterogeneity [@weide_etal_2024; @bruck_esenaliev_2018; @wb_uzb_2018].
